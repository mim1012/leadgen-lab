# Leadgen Lab Marketing Plan

## Purpose

Leadgen Lab should market the repeatable process before marketing the software.

The first market proof is not "can we build a full lead-generation automation platform?" It is "can a small operator use this workflow to launch one compliant campaign experiment faster than they could manually?"

This document defines the launch marketing strategy and keeps a path open for later marketing automation.

## Positioning

Leadgen Lab is a consent-first workbench for launching niche CPA and affiliate landing experiments.

It helps operators turn one campaign idea into:

- a compliant campaign brief,
- a viral landing page concept,
- a static landing page,
- safe CTA and disclaimer copy,
- Threads and SEO content angles,
- keyword ideas,
- tracking and evidence notes.

The public message should avoid "DB scraping", "traffic hacking", or "SNS bypass" language. The safer and more durable message is:

> Launch compliant lead-generation landing experiments faster.

## First Launch Offer

The first public offer should be a service-assisted starter kit, not a full SaaS.

Offer name:

`Leadgen Lab Starter Kit`

Promise:

> Give us one campaign category and we produce a launch-ready landing experiment: page structure, copy, CTA, content calendar, keyword ideas, and compliance checklist.

Initial scope:

- one demo campaign: `moving-quote-call-burden`,
- one static landing page,
- three to five checklist/CTA variants,
- twenty Threads post ideas,
- ten SEO article ideas,
- fifty to one hundred keyword ideas,
- one campaign rules checklist,
- one manual launch and measurement guide.

The tool can be built internally first. The customer-facing product can start as a guided package while the repeatable workflow becomes software.

## Why Not Launch the Full Platform First

"Launch would be delayed" means the learning loop slows down if every future module must be productized before the first customer sees value.

The full PRD includes:

- campaign registry,
- brief generation,
- content planning,
- static site generation,
- tracking,
- evidence capture,
- compliance gates,
- possible database-backed forms,
- future marketing automation.

All of those are useful, but they do not need to be complete before validating the core demand.

The first launch should prove:

- whether people want help creating these funnels,
- whether the moving quote checklist demo converts warm intent into outbound clicks,
- whether operators understand the compliance-first framing,
- whether generated content and pages are good enough to deploy,
- whether users will pay for a campaign setup package.

If the full platform is built first, the team may spend weeks building management, automation, and tracking surfaces before knowing which campaign categories, content formats, and buyer segments actually convert.

## Audience

Primary early audience:

- solo operators exploring CPA or affiliate marketing,
- vibe-coding users looking for monetization projects,
- marketers who can drive traffic but cannot quickly build landing pages,
- developers who can build but need a marketing workflow,
- creators testing Threads, Naver Blog, cafe, or SEO traffic.

Do not start with:

- regulated finance or debt consultation operators,
- health or supplement lead capture,
- insurance-heavy campaigns,
- agencies needing multi-client dashboards.

Those categories may be valuable later, but they add compliance risk before the workflow is proven.

## Channel Strategy

### Threads

Goal:

- build interest through public build logs and practical examples.

Content angles:

- "I turned one CPA campaign idea into a landing experiment."
- "Why plain landing pages lose attention and test-style pages hold it."
- "How to record campaign restrictions before writing copy."
- "A Threads content calendar for one landing experiment."
- "What not to automate in lead generation."

Rules:

- avoid link spam,
- avoid direct promotion of restricted CPA offers,
- show process and examples,
- send interested users to a neutral demo, checklist, or waitlist.

### Naver Blog

Goal:

- capture search intent around vibe coding, affiliate marketing, CPA, landing pages, and content funnels.

Article types:

- tutorials,
- case studies,
- checklist posts,
- template breakdowns,
- compliance-aware campaign setup guides.

Example topics:

- `바이브코딩으로 랜딩페이지 실험 만드는 법`
- `CPA 마케팅 시작 전 캠페인 조건 체크리스트`
- `스레드 유입용 테스트형 랜딩페이지 구조`
- `제휴마케팅 랜딩페이지에 필요한 고지 문구`
- `이사 견적 전화폭탄 줄이는 체크리스트 랜딩 구조`

### Naver Cafe

Goal:

- earn trust by sharing experiments and lessons, not by hard selling.

Post types:

- build-in-public notes,
- lessons learned,
- screenshots of generated demo pages,
- checklist sharing,
- Q&A participation.

Rules:

- respect each cafe's rules,
- do not copy private content into public assets,
- do not automate posting, comments, likes, joins, or DMs,
- keep any capture workflow read-only.

### Free Lead Magnet

Goal:

- collect early interested users and interview candidates.

Recommended assets:

- CPA campaign restriction checklist,
- landing experiment planning sheet,
- Threads content prompt pack,
- UTM naming sheet,
- static HTML demo template.

The lead magnet should not promise income. It should promise a clearer launch workflow.

## Launch Funnel

1. Public content explains the campaign experiment workflow.
2. User downloads a free checklist or views a demo page.
3. User joins waitlist or requests a starter kit.
4. Operator gathers campaign category and restrictions.
5. Leadgen Lab produces one landing experiment package.
6. User manually reviews and launches.
7. Metrics and feedback are collected.
8. Repeated requests become product requirements.

## Manual Before Automated

Automation should follow observed repetition.

Manual first:

- select a campaign category,
- record restrictions,
- define persona,
- generate page copy,
- generate content angles,
- review claims,
- export static page,
- publish content manually,
- review analytics.

Automate later:

- campaign intake form,
- restriction checklist generation,
- persona and brief generation,
- landing page export,
- content calendar generation,
- UTM link generation,
- screenshot evidence capture,
- metric import,
- experiment comparison.

Do not automate:

- account creation,
- posting to platforms without approved APIs,
- comments, likes, follows, DMs,
- bypassing restrictions,
- submitting false leads,
- collecting sensitive personal data without consent and privacy copy.

## Future Marketing Automation Modules

### 1. Content Calendar Generator

Inputs:

- campaign category,
- persona,
- landing angle,
- prohibited claims,
- channel.

Outputs:

- Threads post ideas,
- Naver Blog titles,
- cafe-safe discussion prompts,
- SEO article clusters,
- CTA variants.

### 2. Keyword Expansion Engine

Inputs:

- category,
- geography,
- buyer intent,
- negative keywords,
- compliance restrictions.

Outputs:

- low-cost keyword candidates,
- long-tail SEO topics,
- ad group drafts,
- forbidden keyword warnings.

### 3. UTM and Link Planner

Inputs:

- channel,
- campaign,
- content item,
- CTA destination.

Outputs:

- UTM URL,
- short naming convention,
- tracking checklist,
- outbound click event plan.

### 4. Evidence Capture

Inputs:

- source URL,
- campaign notes,
- published landing URL.

Outputs:

- screenshot,
- visible text archive,
- rule notes,
- generated copy snapshot,
- launch checklist state.

### 5. Performance Review Assistant

Inputs:

- visits,
- outbound clicks,
- click-through rate,
- content source,
- manual notes.

Outputs:

- winning angle summary,
- weak CTA diagnosis,
- next test recommendation,
- pause or continue recommendation.

## Metrics

Pre-revenue metrics:

- checklist downloads,
- waitlist signups,
- demo page views,
- replies and DMs from build-in-public posts,
- user interviews completed.

Starter-kit metrics:

- paid setup requests,
- time from intake to generated package,
- user approval rate,
- launch rate,
- first outbound click event,
- repeat campaign requests.

Product metrics:

- campaigns created,
- landing pages exported,
- content plans generated,
- links tracked,
- experiments compared,
- percentage of campaigns passing compliance checklist.

## 14-Day Marketing Launch Plan

Day 1:

- publish the positioning note,
- create the free campaign restriction checklist.

Day 2:

- publish one build-in-public post about the `moving-quote-call-burden` demo.

Day 3:

- create the landing demo and screenshot assets.

Day 4:

- publish a Naver Blog tutorial on test-style landing pages.

Day 5:

- share a checklist post in relevant communities where allowed.

Day 6:

- publish twenty Threads prompts as a free sample.

Day 7:

- open a small waitlist for five starter-kit users.

Day 8:

- interview two interested users.

Day 9:

- produce one manual starter kit from real user input.

Day 10:

- document the process and gaps.

Day 11:

- publish a case-study style post without private campaign details.

Day 12:

- refine the checklist and content generator prompts.

Day 13:

- ask for one paid pilot or discounted setup.

Day 14:

- decide whether the next build target is content calendar automation, static page generation, or tracking.

## Recommended First Automation Target

The first marketing automation target should be the content calendar generator.

Reason:

- it is useful before deployment,
- it does not require platform account access,
- it avoids risky posting automation,
- it gives immediate user-visible value,
- it produces reusable data for later scheduling or analytics features.

The second target should be UTM and link planning.

The third target should be performance review from manually entered metrics.

Actual posting automation should wait until there are clear API permissions, channel rules, and user approval gates.
